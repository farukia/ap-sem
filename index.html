Conformity in Adolescents

What is Conformity?

Conformity is generally defined as aligning oneself to others in the forms of attitudes, opinions, or behaviors.¹ It is a key component of adolescence and is a key factor in the development of social skills.

The leading mechanism in conformity is reinforcement learning, or using positive and negative outcomes of different scenarios to maximize reward.¹ It involves evaluating certain actions to see what is more socially accepted. Therefore, people are more likely to mimic actions that have previously received positive feedback and act against what has received negative backlash. With unrestricted, nonstop commentary, media makes it easier to conform.

Between the ages of 10 and 12, receptors for oxytocin and dopamine ("happy receptors") multiply in the brain region, making admiration an elevated aspect of their behaviors.² Media is closely tied to this brain region, leading to rushed oxytocin and higher dopamine levels due to its social rewards, making adolescents more susceptible to the effects of media.
